North Carolina is reported to be home to the largest population of Montagnard people outside of Vietnam.\u00a0\u00a0 Montagnards, the indigenous mountain people from Vietnam, experience ongoing oppression and harassment from the Vietnamese government, in part because they helped the U.S. army during the Vietnam War.<\/p>\n

St. Paul\u2019s Christian Church in Raleigh has been a great partner with Uniting NC and is now working with several local Montagnard families to help them reclaim their family names, which they were not allowed to use on their official documents when they immigrated to the U.S. Instead, the Vietnamese government assigned them last names of \u201cA\u201d (for males) or \u201cY\u201d for females. These one-letter names have led to lots of confusion here in the U.S., and resulted in several last names within each family.\u00a0 The legal name-change process is complex and expensive, so in order to help raise funds St. Paul\u2019s Christian Church is hosting several upcoming events.<\/p>\n

This Friday, Sept. 14 there will be a Coffeehouse at 7pm, held on the church campus at 3331 Blue Ridge Rd., Raleigh. For a suggested $5 donation, you can enjoy an evening of musical entertainment, desserts and coffee. Also, on September 23 at 5pm, there will be a screening of the documentary film Abandoned Allies<\/em> , which tells the story of the relationship between the Montagnards and the U.S. Special Forces during the Vietnam War. Reserve your free tickets at www.abandonedalliesspcc.eventbrite.com<\/a>. \u00a0For more information, please contact Rev.
